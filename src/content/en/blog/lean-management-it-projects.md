Born out of Toyota's production system, lean management offers a pragmatic and effective approach to optimising processes, eliminating waste and maximising the value delivered to end users. Let's see how to apply lean management principles to IT project delivery.
![Lean\_Management](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-1.webp)

### Lean management: definition

Initially adopted in manufacturing, lean has gradually established itself across many sectors, notably IT, where it is often combined with agile methodologies such as Scrum or Kanban.

#### What are the basic principles of Lean Management?

![Infographic: the principles of Lean Management](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-2.webp)

Its main goal is to create a smooth, efficient workflow by reducing everything that slows the project down or adds no value. This includes simplifying development processes, optimising collaboration between teams, and delivering working solutions quickly through an iterative and incremental approach.

Projects can thus gain flexibility, reduce time to market and ensure better customer satisfaction. But how do you concretely apply lean principles in an IT project? That is what we will explore by detailing the actions that can be taken at each phase of a project.

### How to apply lean management in the initiation phase?

**The initiation phase (framing and planning)** makes it possible to **clearly define the need, the objectives and the constraints** of the project so as to set the right direction from the outset. This is when the project's **added value** and its alignment with stakeholder expectations are validated.

The **lean management** approach in this phase consists of focusing on the project's **added value**, removing uncertainties and streamlining planning through the main actions below.

#### During a framing activity

Here are the points to observe:

-   Involve the client from the very start of the project in order to identify customer value by analysing real needs, the problems to solve and the expected benefits.
-   Map the value stream (Value Stream Mapping) by identifying critical steps and those that are potential sources of waste.
-   Define the MVP (Minimum Viable Product) to avoid unnecessary features.
-   Encourage rapid experimentation (Lean UX): create prototypes or mock-ups to quickly validate ideas and gather feedback before starting development.
-   Work in a pull flow: developers pull tasks from the backlog when they are ready, which limits multitasking and optimises flow. It also fosters stronger team ownership.
-   Validate continuously throughout development to avoid the tunnel effect and heavy sign-offs at the end of the project.

![](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-3.webp)

#### During a planning activity

The steps to follow as part of a planning activity are as follows:

![](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-4.webp)

-   Prioritise the features with the greatest business impact and value for the end user.
-   Use MoSCoW prioritisation:
    -   **Must**: absolutely necessary
    -   **Should**: important but not critical
    -   **Could**: optional
    -   **Won't**: out of scope for now
-   Avoid over-planning at the start and favour precise short-term planning while keeping an evolving overall vision for the medium and long term, especially in changing environments.
-   Plan in a pull flow and organise work according to actual demand, rather than scheduling tasks in advance (push flow). This adapts the workload to the team's real capacity, avoids overload and optimises operational efficiency.
-   Involve teams in planning: team members take part in estimation, commitment to deadlines and task breakdown, which increases forecast reliability and strengthens ownership. It is important to set aside dedicated slots so as not to spend too much time on it.

### How to apply lean management in the execution phase?

The **execution phase (design & development, validation and steering)** is where the project takes shape: needs are validated, teams develop, test and integrate the planned features. Throughout this phase, steering follow-up is also necessary. The goal: ensure the quality and effectiveness of the project as well as its progress.

Applying **lean management** in the execution phase aims to **optimise value production, reduce waste** and **improve the quality** of software development.

Below are a few examples of lean actions to undertake.

#### During a design activity

During a design activity, applying lean management could consist of:

-   Running co-design workshops (collaborative design).
-   Conducting user interviews to refine understanding of their needs and prioritise the backlog accordingly.
-   Mapping the value stream (Value Stream Mapping) by identifying critical steps and those that are potential sources of waste.
-   Defining the MVP (Minimum Viable Product) to avoid unnecessary features.
-   Encouraging rapid experimentation (Lean UX): creating prototypes or mock-ups to quickly validate ideas and gather feedback before starting development.
-   Working in a pull flow: developers pull tasks from the backlog when they are ready, which limits multitasking and optimises flow. It also fosters stronger team ownership.
-   Validating continuously throughout development to avoid the tunnel effect and heavy sign-offs at the end of the project.

![](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-5.webp)

#### During the development phase

Here are the steps corresponding to applying lean management in the development phase:

![](/assets/images/blog/lean-management-gestion-projet/inline-6.webp)

-   Optimise code and development practices by encouraging pair programming and code reviews to reduce errors upstream.
-   Use the PDCA cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act) to continuously improve processes and code quality.
-   Fix bugs as a priority to avoid the build-up of technical debt and ensure robust code.

#### During validation and deployment activities

For validation and deployment activities, here are the points to consider:

-   Automate tests and deployments (CI/CD) to reduce the cost and time required, but also to detect bugs quickly and accelerate releases.
-   Avoid "Big Bang Delivery" and ship in small continuous iterations rather than waiting until the end of the project.
-   Monitor post-deployment (lean monitoring) and track errors and performance in real time.

#### During steering activities

Finally, for steering activities, lean management involves the following:

-   Adopt visual management with Kanban to track progress while limiting Work In Progress (WIP).
-   Challenge the relevance of recurring meetings.
-   Favour short, effective meetings.
-   Encourage self-organisation and ownership so teams can make quick decisions without bureaucracy.
-   Define relevant indicators at the start of the project that reflect its performance and help make the right decisions.
-   Analyse KPIs to assess the value delivered to the client through feedback and analytics.
-   Hold regular retrospectives to identify areas for improvement with the team and adjust processes.

### How to apply lean management in the closing phase?

The closing phase of a project aims to **officially validate the end of the project**, ensuring that the deliverables meet expectations and that all obligations are fulfilled.

Applying **lean management** during this phase makes it possible to finish the project in an **effective and useful** way, while capitalising on the experience gained to draw lessons for improving future projects.

Below are a few initiatives inspired by lean management:

-   Avoid useless reports or closing documents, and create simple, actionable deliverables for the future (project summary, recommendations, up-to-date functional documentation, etc.).
-   Hold an end-of-project retrospective to improve practices for future projects.
-   Capitalise on experience by creating a concise lessons-learned sheet.
-   Close technical and organisational aspects efficiently with a closing checklist.

### Conclusion

Lean management is a powerful approach to improving efficiency, quality and customer satisfaction in IT project delivery. When properly integrated and adapted to each organisation's specific context, it becomes a catalyst for performance and innovation. At a time when companies must do more with less, lean offers a pragmatic path towards more agile, more reliable and more value-centred projects by transforming the way we work.

In this sense, lean management applied to IT project delivery makes it possible to:

-   Deliver faster and better by focusing on value;
-   Eliminate waste to maximise efficiency and reduce costs;
-   Improve quality and customer satisfaction with regular, tested deliveries;
-   Create a smoother, more motivating work environment for teams.

**"Less waste, more value, a more efficient team and a satisfied client!"** That is the promise of Lean in project management!
