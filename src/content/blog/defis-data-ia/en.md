### Generative AI Use Cases: Virtual Assistants, Marketing And Personalisation

According to recent studies, five generative AI applications stand out clearly in large companies, each offering strong added value for decision-making and the automation of repetitive tasks:

**Code assistants:** the first use case in rapid adoption, with immediate ROI (20% to 50%). These assistants generate and optimise code, create technical documentation and accelerate debugging, radically transforming development team productivity.

**Internal agents and intelligent automation:** business departments are massively deploying these LLM-based assistants. An HR agent analyses hundreds of resumes in seconds, a legal agent summarises complex contracts, while a finance agent automatically generates monthly analysis reports. These use cases improve productivity and decision quality, and apply to a very wide range of roles.

**Internal information synthesis (RAG):** the ability to quickly retrieve, synthesise and present company data is a competitive advantage. RAG systems enable advanced information search and the instant generation of synthetic documents from multiple sources.

**Augmented customer service:** going far beyond FAQs, chatbots now support customers through complex processes (bank loans, insurance) and handle requests in near real time, reducing support costs by 20-30% while improving customer satisfaction.

**Marketing and hyper-personalisation:** beyond simple content creation (articles, visuals, emails), the challenge is to automatically adapt each message to the customer's specific profile and instantly adapt global campaigns into personalised local versions, with considerable production time savings.

All these generative AI applications have one thing in common: they require solid data infrastructure foundations and governance to deliver convincing results.

### Obstacles To Generative AI: 7 Major Challenges To Overcome

When leaders consider integrating generative AI into their business functions, ambitions quickly collide with operational reality because they are generally rather vague and oriented toward results.

Many POCs are carried out, but few become concrete. As Deloitte's 2025 study reveals, **70% of organisations have 30% or less of their POCs reaching maturity within 3 to 6 months**. This rate is comparable to historical POCs (IoT, digital, data), but GenAI concentrates this gap over a much shorter horizon (3 to 6 months versus more than 12 months), which increases the perception of failure.

#### Concretely, How Does It Work?

Data preparation is the fundamental foundation of enterprise generative AI solutions. To use a foundation model without retraining it, the Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) approach is essential.

A RAG is an architecture that extends an LLM by giving it access to indexed company data so that it can use that data in its answers. When an external data source is connected, the data is retrieved, indexed and vectorised so that it can be used by the LLM. This processing, normally time-consuming, is made almost instantaneous by massive processing parallelisation (horizontal scaling), the use of very fast embedding models and intelligent segmentation (smart chunking).

Although it is therefore possible here to use pre-trained, ready-to-use foundation models through direct connection, avoiding the training phase, **the issue of data quality and preparation remains central**. Results are directly linked to the quality level of the input. Foundation models associated with RAG can smooth out certain output defects, but they do not escape this rule.

It is important to understand here that data preparation influences every stage of the AI pipeline: ingestion, indexing, vectorisation, search and generation.

**Impact On Ingestion:**

-   Poorly named or poorly classified files -> chaotic indexing.
-   Multiple versions -> confusion for the retrieval engine.
-   Unsupported and poorly encoded formats -> loss of information.

**Results:** few search results and incomplete information.

**Impact On Vectorisation And Indexing:**

-   Mixed documents (contracts + emails + internal notes in a single PDF).
-   Poor structuring (long, unsegmented files).
-   Irregular text blocks -> less coherent embeddings.

**Impact On Search:**

If the data is:

-   Redundant
-   Contradictory
-   Scattered across multiple files
-   Not normalised

then AI retrieves noise instead of relevant information, even if GPT-5 or another high-performing model is running behind it.

**Results:** hallucinations, vague answers, incorrect citations.

**Impact On Generation:**

Final generation depends intrinsically on the quality of the source data. Poor data leads to poor answers. Clean and well-structured data leads to reliable, relevant and contextualised answers.

### Data Security And Governance To Develop Generative AI

Data security is a major point of attention in generative AI projects. Traditionally, companies manage their data in silos, with strict access policies and centralised governance at CIO level, or sometimes with a simple lack of governance.

To make a GenAI initiative concrete, the approach relies on the opposite principle: gathering and indexing heterogeneous data to feed the model. Governance must also be solid, with correctly classified data, the right security metadata and controlled fragmentation across the company's different tools.

Controlled data flow management is essential to avoid leakage of data exposed to the model.

The main risks include:

-   **Internal leaks:** the data used to train the model must not be distributed to people who are not authorised to consult it.
-   **External leaks:** exposing models through external APIs or cloud services creates vulnerabilities, particularly in sectors such as healthcare or banking where regulatory requirements differ.
-   **Vulnerability to adversarial attacks:** agentic AI, capable of autonomous actions, introduces new attack surfaces such as prompt injection or model poisoning.

To address these issues, the zero trust approach is becoming the standard. Advanced RAG systems now include "pre-prompt layers", "context isolation" and "post-retrieval filtering" to reprocess initial prompts and compartmentalise requests. Features provide solutions, but data governance management remains essential across all use cases.

Therefore, before deploying GenAI at scale, technical and governance prerequisites are essential to guarantee lasting reliability adapted to sector-specific requirements.

### The Foundations Of A Data-Driven Company: Data, Integration And Governance

Artificial intelligence in business is no longer just an option, but a strategic necessity. However, there is a significant gap between ambitions and the concrete delivery of AI projects. Assessing data maturity allows organisations, particularly mid-sized companies, to understand the fundamentals required for success.

It is important to keep in mind that each data architecture is different depending on use cases and company topology. However, there are questions that must always be addressed, whatever the context. In particular, we can mention the importance of putting in place:

-   **Reliable and accessible data:** the quality and accessibility of data (internal and external) are the foundation of any AI project, with metadata management playing a central role in classification and security.
-   **High-performing infrastructure:** computing power, massive storage and scalability, generally through cloud solutions.
-   **Solid governance:** GDPR compliance, traceability, clear management of access rights and an "AI Security Framework" derived from zero trust, with data lineage extended to AI.
-   **Specialised skills:** data engineers, data architects, data analysts, MLOps, data scientists/AI engineers, organisational leaders, data managers/data stewards. A data team must be sufficiently structured and able to evolve according to needs.
-   **Smooth integration:** AI models must interface with existing systems (CRM, ERP, e-commerce, etc.) to feed themselves, which requires APIs.

### Data Maturity And AI Integration: Assessing Your Level To Scale Up

When a lack of results is identified, initiatives are often stopped or deprioritised. **It is therefore essential to assess maturity and put iterative improvement processes in place.** To position yourself, let us define the main maturity levels found in companies:

#### Level 1: Fragmentation And Improvisation

Data is scattered across a multitude of files and tools (Excel, various drives, departmental silos, etc.). There is no governance in place and no shared repository. This maturity level implies frequent errors, slow access to information and low scalability. At this stage, the company remains highly artisanal in its data management.

#### Level 2: Partial Centralisation

A first step is taken with **the introduction of certain data centralisation tools**, typically a data warehouse or data lake. **Governance begins to appear**. The idea is to have a single repository for key data (customers, sales, etc.). However, centralisation is often incomplete: some departments continue to work independently. Data quality improves, but governance remains limited.

#### Level 3: Modern And Governed Infrastructure

The company reaches a new stage by **modernising** its architectures, often through cloud or hybrid models. **Structured data** (tables, SQL databases) as well as **unstructured data** (text, images, logs, etc.) are taken into account. We find:

-   **Automated data pipelines** to secure flows,
-   Data is cleaned and of good **quality/reliability**,
-   **Governance rules are established** (catalogues, master repositories, access policies),
-   **Data and functional teams are trained** to ensure consistency and begin implementing MLOps and the first AI use cases.

#### Level 4: Data-Driven Organisation And Scalable Architecture

At the highest level, data becomes a true product. Business teams access it directly through documented and governed **data products**. Architectures rely on modern approaches such as **Data Fabric** or **Data Mesh**, connected to all sources (SaaS, IoT, on-premise databases, etc.). Decisions are made using reliable and available data, and **AI is integrated into key processes**.

Of course, these levels are schematic. Nevertheless, this framework helps assess where you stand and what the next step is to progress. Many companies today realise they are stagnating at an insufficient maturity level (1 or 2) compared with their AI ambitions. Realistically, integrating GenAI for large-scale enterprise use requires at least a maturity level of 3 to guarantee sufficient reliability and results.

### FAQ On Generative AI

**What Is The Best Generative AI For A Company?**

There is no single universal "best" generative AI. Rather, the best one is the one that naturally integrates with the company's existing digital environment. In 2026, leaders such as OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Anthropic and Mistral offer AI tools with differentiated capabilities. For small businesses, the challenge is to select a solution adapted to their specific needs rather than searching for the most technically powerful one. AI's potential lies in how well it matches your usage context.

**What Are The Different Types Of Generative AI?**

Generative AI is classified into several categories depending on the modalities it processes. Textual models (LLMs) such as GPT-5 or Mistral produce written content, code and analyses. Visual models are used to design images (Midjourney, DALL-E) or videos. Voice and music are produced by audio models, while multimodal models combine several types of data. Generative AI is now used in every field: code assistants, conversational agents, document synthesis tools, marketing content creators and specialised business assistants. Each form of AI has specific potential depending on the company's needs, ranging from process optimisation to the creation of new offers.
