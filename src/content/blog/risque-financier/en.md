**The world of finance is in constant evolution, driven by markets, monetary policy and investor behaviour. For newcomers, it can be hard to navigate this complex universe, whether you are an individual or a company seeking to secure your investments. That is why this introductory article on the typology of financial risks, designed for a beginner audience, offers an overview of the main concepts and mechanisms related to risk.**

![Meritis - financial risks - introduction](/assets/images/blog/risque-financier/inline-1.webp)

Through an educational approach and concrete examples, we will try to understand its foundations, measurement methods, challenges and consequences in real situations.

### What is a financial risk?

Before even talking about financial risk, it is essential to set out a clear definition of what a risk is. In economics as elsewhere, risk is defined as exposure to a potential loss, associated with an uncertain event. Two elements lie at the heart of this notion:

-   The possibility of suffering a loss (the exposure);
-   And the probability that the feared event will occur (the uncertainty).

By combining these two parameters, we can assess the reality of a risk. In the case of market risk, it more specifically refers to a risk of financial loss linked to an unfavourable variation on the financial markets. This simple definition introduces the various forms this type of risk can take.

### What are the main types of risk?

#### Market risk

Market risk itself is divided into four sub-categories:

-   **Equity risk:** corresponds to the fall in value of the share (ownership title) held by an investor.
-   **Currency risk (Forex – _Foreign Exchange Market_):** concerns fluctuations between currencies, such as the euro against the dollar.
-   **Commodity risk:** arises from price variations on products such as oil, gold, wheat or metals, often caused by geopolitical or climate events.
-   **Interest-rate risk:** occurs when a central bank changes its key rates, affecting the yields of bond products.

To better understand the latter, consider an investor who buys a bond at 3% over ten years. If, a year later, the key rate rises to 4%, their bond becomes less attractive. They will then prefer to sell their position to acquire a bond offering a better yield. This loss of attractiveness is the core of interest-rate risk.

#### Liquidity risk

Liquidity risk can take two forms:

1.  **Equity liquidity risk:** it arises when an investor cannot find a buyer for an asset they wish to sell — for example, the fall in a share's value, sometimes forcing a sale at a loss. A recent example is Tesla shares, massively sold by worried investors, causing a drop in the Tesla share price.
2.  **Bank liquidity risk:** on a more global scale, it materialises when a bank no longer has enough cash to meet its clients' demand. This is what happened in 2008 with the collapse of Lehman Brothers, where massive client withdrawals exposed the institution's inability to meet its obligations.

#### Credit risk

Credit risk concerns a direct relationship between a lender (often a bank) and a borrower (individual, company or state). It is the risk of non-payment at maturity in a credit operation.

#### Counterparty risk

Counterparty risk occurs only on the over-the-counter market (bilateral transaction). It is the risk that the debtor fails to honour its obligations, leading to a risk of non-delivery of the asset. Unlike the organised market, the over-the-counter market has no clearing house, which makes this risk more acute.

#### Operational risk

It is linked to human error, process failures or external events. It can be involuntary or, in some cases, voluntary. When intentional, it constitutes fraud. An emblematic case remains that of Jérôme Kerviel in 2008, a trader at Société Générale, who had concealed massive speculative positions for several years. This fraud resulted in a colossal loss for the bank at the time of the subprime crisis.

#### Compliance risk

It stems from the failure to comply with legal, regulatory or ethical rules. It can lead to administrative, financial or judicial sanctions, and generally translates into losses for the company concerned.

### What is the difference between financial risks and banking risks?

It is important to distinguish **market risk** from **banking risk**, even though both are closely linked to the overall functioning of the financial system. Banking risks bring together a set of threats specific to the activity of financial institutions, such as:

-   **Credit risk** (linked to the non-repayment of a loan);
-   **Liquidity risk** (inability to meet client withdrawal demands);
-   **Operational risk** (human error, technical failures or fraud).

Unlike market risk, which arises from external factors linked to financial-market fluctuations, banking risks are directly linked to internal practices, asset-liability management, and compliance with the regulatory constraints specific to the banking business.

### How to measure financial risk? Which indicators?

There are several methods, including two tools commonly used to assess the magnitude and probability of a market risk materialising.

#### Value at Risk (VaR)

The first, Value at Risk (VaR), is a statistical indicator that measures, over a given period, the extent of the risk and the probability that financial losses will occur.

[![VaR illustration](/assets/images/blog/risque-financier/inline-2.webp)](https://meritis.fr/blog/vous-saurez-tout-sur-la-var/)

#### Beta

The second measurement tool is beta, an indicator that measures the volatility of a financial instrument based on its past performance relative to the market. Beta is generally between 0 and 1:

-   A beta equal to 1 means the asset moves at the same pace as the market.
-   If it is greater than 1, it is more volatile than the market. This is the case for start-ups, very sensitive to market movements.
-   Conversely, public companies generally have a beta below 1, reflecting stability.
-   A beta below 0 even indicates that the asset moves in the opposite direction to the market.

### Credit risk vs counterparty risk: two notions not to be confused

Often confused, these two risk notions must nevertheless be distinguished:

-   **Credit risk** concerns a direct relationship between a lender (often a bank) and a borrower (individual, company or state). It is the risk of non-payment at maturity in a credit operation.
-   **Counterparty risk**, on the other hand, occurs only on the over-the-counter market (bilateral transaction). It is the risk that the debtor fails to honour its obligations, leading to a risk of non-delivery of the asset. Unlike the organised market, the over-the-counter market has no clearing house (an intermediary that secures operations), which makes this risk more acute.

### How are financial markets structured?

#### Primary market vs secondary market

-   **The primary market** refers to the place where securities are issued for the first time, for example during an IPO (Initial Public Offering) or a fundraising round.
-   **The secondary market** represents the market for the subsequent exchange of these securities between investors.

#### Organised market vs over-the-counter market

-   **Organised market:** like Euronext, it is regulated and standardised. Transactions are secured by a clearing house.
-   **Over-the-counter market (OTC):** the parties know each other and jointly define the terms of the transaction. Counterparty risk is mainly present on this type of market.

### The role of the clearing house

On the organised market, the clearing house plays a central role. In France, the main institution is LCH, which acts between buyer and seller to guarantee the proper execution of transactions. Its main missions are:

-   Record orders;
-   Verify the solvency of the parties;
-   Validate the terms of the transaction;
-   Calculate net balances (clearing);
-   Integrate related operations (netting);
-   Require (where applicable) collateral deposits (margin calls);
-   Supervise the delivery of securities and the settlement of funds (on T+2).

### How to map financial risks?

In the banking sector, financial-risk management is at the heart of soundness and trust. Risk mapping makes it possible to identify, prioritise and monitor the threats likely to affect a bank's financial stability.

**The steps to map risks:**

1.  Identify the types of risk: credit risk, market risk, operational risk, etc.;
2.  Assess the criticality of each risk. It is possible to project from historical data, then determine the potential consequences of each risk;
3.  Create a risk dashboard to have a visual matrix;
4.  Put appropriate management strategies in place to reduce the impact of the risk.

#### Examples of financial-risk management strategies

-   Secure internal processes;
-   Diversify assets;
-   Hedge exposures;
-   Use securitisation.

Risk management relies on **rigorous governance**, **real-time monitoring** and **continuous adaptation** to market developments or regulatory changes.

### Three concrete examples of financial risks

To better anchor the notions covered, three financial crises, each linked to a type of risk, are analysed.

#### The collapse of Silicon Valley Bank (2023)

Silicon Valley Bank mainly collected deposits from start-ups. After a massive wave of deposits, the bank invested in fixed-rate bonds. When the Fed raised its key rates, the value of these bonds fell. Faced with a bank run, the bank had to resell its bonds at a loss to honour withdrawals, combining:

-   Interest-rate risk;
-   Equity risk;
-   Bank liquidity risk.

#### The COVID-19 health crisis (March 2020)

The CAC 40 fell by around 38% in a few days. This drop illustrated:

-   A sudden **equity risk**;
-   An **interest-rate risk** induced by the ECB's response;
-   A **commodity risk**, linked to the post-lockdown recovery and inflation.

#### The subprime crisis (2008)

The Fed long kept rates low, encouraging access to credit. When rates rose, many American households could no longer repay, causing:

-   A **credit risk**;
-   A **liquidity risk** on the markets;
-   A **market risk** through the fall of credit-backed assets;
-   A global **systemic risk**.

### Conclusion: why understanding financial risks is essential

Financial-risk management is a **fundamental pillar** for any economic player. Understanding their nature, their measurement and their impact through concrete cases makes it possible to better anticipate market hazards.

Whether you are a beginner or experienced, learning about these typologies is a key step towards better mastery of financial dynamics.
